Networking Basics

⬡
Networking is simply devices talking to each other to share data.

The Highway Analogy: Think of a city's transportation system. Houses are the computers, roads are the physical cables or Wi-Fi waves, and the cars driving between them are the data packets being shared.

💻 Node & Host

  • Node: Any physical device connected to the network (e.g., router, switch, printer).
  • Host: A specific smart node that has an IP address and runs applications (e.g., your laptop or a web server).

📦 The Data Packet

Files aren't sent across the internet as one giant chunk. A 5MB photo is chopped up into thousands of tiny digital envelopes called Packets.

🤝 Client-Server & P2P Models

  • Client-Server: The Client asks for information (You opening YouTube). The Server is a powerful, always-on computer that holds the video and serves it back to you.
  • Peer-to-Peer (P2P): Devices talk directly to each other without a central server. Everyone is both a client and a server simultaneously.

Network Types (Scope)

Networks are categorized primarily by the geographical distance they cover and their specific purpose.

🏠 LAN (Local Area Network)

A network contained in a single physical location. Your house, a coffee shop, or an office building is a LAN. Devices share a single Router to reach the outside world.

🌍 WAN (Wide Area Network)

A massive network spanning vast distances. A WAN is essentially thousands of LANs connected together via massive telecom cables. The Internet is the ultimate WAN.

📏 Other Notable Scopes

  • PAN (Personal Area Network): A tiny network around a single person. Example: Your phone connected to Bluetooth earbuds.
  • MAN (Metropolitan Area Network): Covers an entire city or a massive university campus.
  • SAN (Storage Area Network): A highly specialized network used by data centers strictly for connecting servers to hard drives.

Physical Cabling

Even in a wireless world, Wi-Fi hits a physical wire eventually. Here is what carries the world's data.

🔌 Copper Twisted-Pair (Ethernet)›


Uses electrical signals to transmit 1s and 0s. The twisting cancels out electromagnetic interference.

  • Categories: Cat5e, Cat6, Cat6a. Higher numbers mean faster speeds.
  • Limitation: Signal degrades after exactly 100 meters (328 feet).
✨ Fiber Optic›


Uses pulses of light sent through microscopic strands of glass. The backbone of the modern internet.

  • Single-mode Fiber: Uses high-powered Lasers. Can travel across oceans without losing signal strength.
  • Superpower: 100% immune to electrical interference and water damage.
📺 Coaxial Cable›


Thick cable with a single copper pin in the center. Used heavily by cable companies to deliver internet from the street to your house.

Network Topologies

Topology is the architectural map of how devices are physically wired together.

⭐ Star Topology›


Concept: All devices connect individually to a central switch. This is the standard for modern homes and offices.

  • Pros: Highly manageable. If one computer's cable breaks, the rest are fine.
  • Cons: Single point of failure. If the central switch dies, the entire network drops.
🕸️ Mesh Topology›


Concept: Every device has a direct, dedicated cable connecting to every other device on the network.

  • Pros: Unbeatable fault tolerance. Data automatically routes around damage.
  • Cons: Incredibly expensive. Used strictly for core internet infrastructure.
⭕ Legacy Topologies (Ring & Bus)›


Bus: All devices tap into one backbone cable. Cheap, but massive data collisions.

Ring: Devices are in a closed circle. If one computer dies, the ring breaks.

Network Devices

⬡ Switch (Local)

Connects devices within the same local network (LAN). It learns exactly which computer's MAC address is plugged into which physical port, sending data only where it belongs.

⟿ Router (Global)

Connects different networks together (WAN). It reads IP addresses and calculates the absolute fastest path to get data across the globe.

🔌 Modems & Access Points

  • Modem: Translates specific analog signals from the street (Coaxial/Telephone) into digital 1s and 0s.
  • Access Point (AP): Receives invisible Wi-Fi radio waves and converts them into electrical signals in an Ethernet cable.

Protocols & Data Delivery

⊟

What is a protocol? A Protocol is a strict, mathematical set of rules defining exactly how data is formatted and exchanged between computers.

📦 TCP (Reliable)

Transmission Control Protocol. It guarantees delivery via a "3-Way Handshake" and resends lost data. Used for Emails and Web browsing.

🚀 UDP (Fast)

User Datagram Protocol. Favors speed over reliability. Throws data instantly and drops corrupted packets. Used for Live Video and Gaming.

🌐 Common Application Protocols

  • HTTP/HTTPS: The language of the Web. The 'S' stands for Secure (encrypted).
  • FTP: File Transfer Protocol for large files.
  • SSH: Secure Shell for command-line server access.

IP, MAC & Ports

🏷️ MAC Address (Physical)

  • Permanent: Burned into your device at the factory. Never changes.
  • Scope: Used strictly by Switches inside your LAN.

📍 IP Address (Logical)

  • Temporary: Changes if you move from home to a coffee shop.
  • Scope: Used by Routers to navigate globally.

🚪 Port Numbers

If the IP Address gets the data to your computer, the Port Number gets it to the correct software application (e.g., Port 443 for HTTPS Web, Port 22 for SSH).

Public vs. Private IPs (NAT)

🎭

NAT (Network Address Translation): Your router acts as a receptionist. You have one Public phone number for the building, but private extensions for every desk inside.

🏡 Private IP

  • Invisible and unreachable from the outside internet.
  • Starts with 192.168.x.x or 10.x.x.x.

🌐 Public IP

  • Used globally on the WAN (Internet).
  • You only have ONE per household, assigned by your ISP.

Subnetting Basics

🎭 The Subnet Mask

An IP address tells you the house, but the Subnet Mask (like 255.255.255.0) acts like a digital highlighter.

  • It highlights which part represents the Network (The Neighborhood).
  • It highlights which part represents the Host (The Specific House).

📈 Why Subnet?

  • Performance: Reduces broadcast traffic by creating smaller groups.
  • Security: Allows routers to strictly block departments from talking to each other.

🔢 CIDR Notation

Instead of writing 255.255.255.0, engineers use 192.168.1.1/24. The /24 locks in 24 bits as the network portion.

DHCP & DNS (Services)

🎫 DHCP (The Receptionist)

Dynamic Host Configuration Protocol. When you connect to Wi-Fi, you don't type an IP manually; DHCP hands you a temporary one automatically.

📖 DNS (The Phonebook)

Domain Name System. Computers route data using numbers. Humans prefer words (google.com). DNS translates human words into computer IPs instantly.

Network Metrics & Quality

  • Bandwidth: The absolute maximum potential capacity of a cable.
  • Throughput: The actual, real-world speed you get after traffic jams and protocol overhead.
  • Latency (Ping): The time delay it takes for data to travel. Measured in milliseconds (ms).
  • Jitter: The fluctuation in Latency. Ruins VoIP phone calls.
  • Packet Loss: When data packets are dropped in transit. Causes stuttering video.

The OSI Model Layers

🥞

How a click becomes electricity. The OSI model is a 7-layer theoretical blueprint explaining the exact journey data takes from your screen, down into physical electricity, and back up.

7 Application (HTTP)›


Where applications interact with the network.

6 Presentation (Encryption)›


Formats and encrypts data (SSL/TLS).

5 Session (Connections)›


Manages and gracefully closes connections.

4 Transport (TCP/Ports)›


Chops data into Segments and assigns Port Numbers.

3 Network (Routers/IP)›


Adds IP Addresses to create Packets. Calculates global routes.

2 Data Link (Switches/MAC)›


Adds MAC Addresses for local delivery.

1 Physical (Cables/Wi-Fi)›


Converts digital 1s and 0s into electrical pulses or light.

The Cloud & Data Centers

☁️

There is no cloud, it's just someone else's computer. When you save a photo to "The Cloud," it lands on a massive physical hard drive inside a remote warehouse.

🏭 Data Centers & VMs

Instead of running one operating system per physical server, they use Virtual Machines (VMs). Software tricks the hardware into thinking it's 10 different computers.

📦 Cloud Service Models

  • SaaS: Software as a Service (Netflix, Gmail).
  • PaaS: Platform as a Service (Heroku).
  • IaaS: Infrastructure as a Service (AWS EC2).

Internet of Things (IoT)

💡 What is IoT?

Putting a network chip in things that traditionally weren't computers. Smart lightbulbs, pacemakers, and cars. Because of this, we ran out of IPv4 addresses and had to move to IPv6.

🧠 Edge Computing

Processing data locally at the "Edge" of the network (like inside the car itself) instead of sending it all to the Cloud. Faster and safer.

⚡ Quick Check

Network Security Basics

🧱 Firewalls

The bouncer at the door. Stateful Firewalls are smart: if you request a webpage, they open a temporary hole for the reply, but block uninvited traffic.

🛡️ Common Threats

  • Phishing: Fake emails tricking humans.
  • DDoS: Flooding a server with fake traffic so it crashes.
  • MitM: Intercepting messages between two parties.

VPNs & Proxies

🚇 VPN (Virtual Private Network)

The Armored Tunnel. A VPN wraps all your traffic in encryption and routes it remotely. Your local ISP can only see gibberish.

🕵️ Proxy Servers

The Middleman. You send a request to the proxy, it fetches the webpage, and sends it back. Doesn't heavily encrypt your whole connection like a VPN.

Troubleshooting Tools

🏓 Ping

Sends an ICMP echo request and waits for a reply. It verifies basic connectivity and shows latency.

🗺️ Traceroute (tracert)

Maps the exact journey. Shows every Router (hop) your data travels through. Shows exactly which router dropped the ball.

🔍 ipconfig / ifconfig

Reveals your current Private IP, Subnet Mask, and Default Gateway (Router IP).

🛠️ nslookup & netstat

nslookup: Tests your DNS server.
netstat: Shows active TCP connections to find what apps are phoning home.
